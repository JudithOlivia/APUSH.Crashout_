Unit 4: 1800–1848

Key Concept 4.1: The United States began to develop a modern democracy and celebrated a new national culture, while Americans sought to define the nation's democratic ideals and change their society and institutions to match them.

I. The Rise of Political Democracy

The "Revolution of 1800": Thomas Jefferson's election

  • First peaceful transfer of power between rival political parties (Federalists to Democratic-Republicans)
  • Proved the Constitution and the republic could survive

Jefferson's Presidency (1801-1809):

  • Philosophy: Agrarian republic, "wise and frugal government," strict constructionism
  • Contradictions: Maintained many Federalist programs (Bank of the U.S.), and his Louisiana Purchase (1803) used implied powers (a loose constructionist argument!)

Expansion of the Franchise (Voting Rights):

  • By the 1820s-1830s, most states eliminated property requirements for white male voters
  • Rise of universal white male suffrage – the "common man" became a powerful political force

The Second Party System (Jacksonian Era):

  • Democrats (Andrew Jackson): Favored limited government, states' rights, and the "common man." Suspicious of banks and modernization
  • Whigs (Henry Clay): Favored a strong federal government, the American System, and industrial/commercial development

II. The Development of an American Culture

  • Hudson River School: First coherent American art movement, focused on majestic American landscapes
  • Transcendentalism (Romanticism): Emphasized individualism, emotion, and intuition over reason
    • Key Figures: Ralph Waldo Emerson ("Self-Reliance"), Henry David Thoreau ("Civil Disobedience")
  • Utopian Communities: Brook Farm, Oneida, Shakers – experimental communities seeking perfect societies

III. Social & Political Reform Movements

Driven by the Second Great Awakening and democratic ideals

  • Temperance Movement: Aimed to reduce/eliminate alcohol consumption. Led by women and preachers
  • Public Education: Horace Mann advocated for tax-supported, mandatory public schools to create educated citizens
  • Abolitionism:
    • Gradualists: Wanted slow end to slavery with compensation
    • Immediatists (William Lloyd Garrison): Demanded immediate, uncompensated emancipation. Published The Liberator
    • Black Abolitionists: Frederick Douglass (powerful orator and writer), David Walker, Sojourner Truth
  • Women's Rights:
    • Cult of Domesticity: Prevailing ideal that women's role was in the home as moral guardians
    • Seneca Falls Convention (1848): Organized by Elizabeth Cady Stanton and Lucretia Mott. Issued the "Declaration of Sentiments" modeled on the Declaration of Independence, demanding equal rights for women, including suffrage
Key Concept 4.2: Innovations in technology, agriculture, and commerce powerfully accelerated the American economy, precipitating profound changes to U.S. society and to national and regional identities.

I. The Market Revolution

A major shift from a subsistence economy to a market-based economy connected by transportation and communication networks

Causes:

  • Transportation: Canals (Erie Canal), railroads, steamboats
  • Communication: Telegraph (Samuel Morse)
  • Agriculture: Cotton Gin (Eli Whitney) → made cotton highly profitable, revitalizing Southern slavery
  • Industry: Interchangeable parts (Whitney again), factory system (Lowell System)

Effects:

  • Increased Regional Specialization:
    • North: Industrial, manufacturing
    • South: "Cotton Kingdom," plantation agriculture
    • West: The "breadbasket," grain and livestock
  • Growth of Cities: Urban centers swelled, especially in the North
  • Changes for Labor: Shift from skilled artisans to wage workers in factories. Early labor unions began to form

II. The Age of Jackson (1829-1837)

Jacksonian Democracy: Champion of the "common man," but strongly pro-slavery and anti-Native American

Key Policies:

  • Spoils System: Rewarding political supporters with government jobs
  • Indian Removal Act (1830): Forced relocation of Native American tribes (Cherokee, Creek, etc.) west of the Mississippi River. Trail of Tears (1838) was the deadly consequence
  • Nullification Crisis (1832-1833): South Carolina declared a federal tariff "null and void." Jackson threatened force, upholding federal power. Crisis highlighted growing sectional tensions over states' rights
  • Bank War: Jackson vetoed the recharter of the Second Bank of the United States, believing it was a corrupt monopoly that favored the elite. He "killed" the bank by moving federal funds to state "pet banks," contributing to the Panic of 1837
Key Concept 4.3: The U.S. interest in increasing foreign trade and expanding its national borders shaped the nation's foreign policy and spurred government and private initiatives.

I. Territorial Expansion & Manifest Destiny

Manifest Destiny: The belief that God intended the U.S. to expand its dominion and spread democracy across the entire North American continent

  • Louisiana Purchase (1803): Jefferson bought from France, doubled the size of the U.S.
  • Florida (1819): Acquired through the Adams-Onís Treaty with Spain
  • Texas (1845): Annexed after American settlers revolted against Mexico
  • Oregon Country (1846): "Fifty-Four Forty or Fight!" – treaty with Britain established current northern border
  • Mexican-American War (1846-1848):
    • Cause: Border dispute (Rio Grande vs. Nueces River) and U.S. desire for Mexican land
    • Opposition: Whigs (like Abraham Lincoln) and abolitionists saw it as a pro-slavery land grab
    • Result: U.S. victory. Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo gave U.S. the Mexican Cession (CA, NV, UT, AZ, NM, CO)
    • Impact: Reopened fierce debate over the expansion of slavery into the new territories

II. Foreign Policy & National Identity

War of 1812 (1812-1815):

  • Causes: British impressment of U.S. sailors, interference with American trade, desire for western expansion
  • Key Events: Burning of Washington D.C., Battle of New Orleans (made Andrew Jackson a hero)
  • Results: "Era of Good Feelings" – surge of American nationalism, death of the Federalist Party

Monroe Doctrine (1823):

  • Declared the Western Hemisphere closed to future European colonization
  • The U.S. would stay out of European affairs
  • A bold statement of American nationalism on the world stage

Key Religious & Social Context

Second Great Awakening (1790s-1840s):

  • A wave of religious revivalism that emphasized emotional, passionate conversion
  • Key Idea: Perfectionism – individuals and society can be perfected through faith and effort
  • Impact: Fueled the major reform movements (abolition, temperance, women's rights, etc.)

Key Themes for Unit 4:

  • American & National Identity: The rise of Jacksonian democracy and Manifest Destiny
  • Politics & Power: The battles over the bank, tariffs, and states' rights
  • Work, Exchange, & Technology: The transformative Market Revolution
  • Culture & Society: The reform movements inspired by the Second Great Awakening
  • Migration & Settlement: Westward expansion and its consequences
  • America in the World: The Monroe Doctrine and territorial expansion through war and diplomacy
