Unit 6: 1865–1898

Key Concept 6.1: Technological advances, large-scale production methods, and the opening of new markets encouraged the rise of industrial capitalism in the United States.

I. The Rise of Industrial Capitalism

Causes of Industrial Growth:

  • Natural Resources: Abundant coal, iron, timber, and oil.
  • Government Support: Laissez-faire policies, protective tariffs, land grants to railroads, and a pro-business Supreme Court.
  • New Inventions: Bessemer process (steel), telegraph, telephone (Alexander Graham Bell), electric light bulb (Thomas Edison).

The Business Revolution:

  • Corporations & Finance: Rise of large corporations funded by the sale of stock. Stock markets like the NYSE became powerful.
  • Captains of Industry / Robber Barons:
    • John D. Rockefeller: Standard Oil Company – used horizontal integration and the trust to create a monopoly.
    • Andrew Carnegie: Carnegie Steel – used vertical integration to control all aspects of production.
    • J.P. Morgan: Banker who financed and consolidated major corporations.
  • The Gospel of Wealth: Carnegie's philosophy that the wealthy have a moral obligation to use their wealth for the public good (philanthropy).

II. The Labor Movement

  • National Labor Union (NLU), Knights of Labor: Open to all workers (skilled, unskilled, women, African Americans). Goals: 8-hour day, worker cooperatives.
    • Conditions for Workers: Long hours, low pay, dangerous conditions, child labor.
    • Early Unions:
      • National Labor Union (NLU), Knights of Labor: Open to all workers (skilled, unskilled, women, African Americans). Goals: 8-hour day, worker cooperatives.
      • American Federation of Labor (AFL): Led by Samuel Gompers. Only for skilled workers. Focused on "bread and butter" issues: higher wages, shorter hours, better conditions.
    • Major Labor Conflicts:
      • Great Railroad Strike (1877): First major national labor uprising.
      • Haymarket Affair (1886): Bomb thrown at a labor rally in Chicago. Associated unions with anarchism and violence, hurting the labor movement.
      • Homestead Strike (1892): Steelworkers vs. Carnegie Steel. Violent defeat for the union.
      • Pullman Strike (1894): Railroad workers led by Eugene V. Debs. Federal government used an injunction to break the strike, showing government support for business over labor.
    Key Concept 6.2: The migrations that accompanied industrialization transformed both urban and rural areas of the United States and caused dramatic social and cultural change.

    I. Mass Migration & Urbanization

    • "New Immigration" (1880s-1920s): Shift from Northern/Western Europe to Southern and Eastern Europe (Italy, Poland, Russia, Greece).
    • Urbanization: Rapid growth of cities due to immigration and internal migration (farmers, African Americans).
    • Urban Problems: Overcrowded tenements, disease, pollution, crime, and inadequate sanitation.
    • Political Machines: Corrupt organizations that controlled city politics (e.g., Tammany Hall in NYC). Provided services to immigrants and the poor in exchange for votes.

    II. The "New South" & The West

    The "New South":

    • Vision: Promoted by Henry Grady – an industrialized, diversified South.
    • Reality: Remained largely agricultural and poor. Sharecropping and tenant farming kept many (black and white) in debt.
    • Jim Crow Segregation: Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) established the "separate but equal" doctrine, legalizing racial segregation.

    Westward Expansion:

    • Homestead Act (1862): Offered 160 acres of free land to settlers, encouraging westward migration.
    • Transcontinental Railroad (1869): Connected the coasts, facilitated settlement and trade. Built by Union Pacific (west) and Central Pacific (east, using Chinese labor).
    • Conflict with Native Americans:
      • Battle of Little Bighorn (1876): Native victory (Custer's Last Stand).
      • Wounded Knee (1890): Massacre of Sioux by U.S. cavalry, marking the end of the Indian Wars.
    • Assimilation Policy: Dawes Act (1887) aimed to break up tribal lands and force assimilation by granting individual plots. Resulted in massive loss of Native land.
    Key Concept 6.3: The Gilded Age produced new cultural and intellectual movements, public reform efforts, and political debates over economic and social policies.

    I. Responses to Industrialization & Inequality

    • Social Darwinism: Applied Charles Darwin's "survival of the fittest" to society. Justified the vast wealth of industrialists and opposed government intervention to help the poor.
    • Reform Movements:
      • Social Gospel: Religious movement that applied Christian ethics to social problems (poverty, inequality). Led to the creation of settlement houses.
      • Settlement Houses: Jane Addams' Hull House in Chicago provided services to immigrants and the urban poor.
    • Populist Movement:
      • Causes: Economic hardship for farmers – falling prices, high railroad shipping rates, debt.
      • Granger Movement & Farmers' Alliances: Organized farmers to fight for their economic interests.
      • Populist (People's) Party (1892):
        • Platform: Free silver (unlimited coinage of silver to create inflation), graduated income tax, government ownership of railroads, 8-hour workday.
      • Election of 1896: The Populists backed Democratic candidate William Jennings Bryan ("Cross of Gold" speech). His defeat by William McKinley (pro-gold standard) marked the collapse of the Populist Party, but many of their ideas were later adopted.

    II. Intellectual & Cultural Movements

    • Realism & Naturalism: In literature and art, focused on depicting everyday life and the harsh realities of society (Mark Twain, Stephen Crane).
    • Conservation: Early movement to protect natural resources, led by figures like John Muir (founder of the Sierra Club).

    Key Themes for Unit 6:

    • American & National Identity: Debates over wealth, poverty, and the meaning of the "American Dream" in an industrial age.
    • Politics & Power: The rise of big business and its influence on government. The protest politics of farmers and workers.
    • Work, Exchange, & Technology: The transformative power of industrialization and the corporate consolidation of the economy.
    • Culture & Society: The massive social changes brought by immigration, urbanization, and the struggle for rights.
    • Migration & Settlement: The final settlement of the West and the great wave of "new immigration."
    • Geography & Environment: The environmental impact of industrialization and western settlement.
