II. The Age of Jackson (1829-1837)

Jacksonian Democracy: Champion of the "common man," but strongly pro-slavery and anti-Native American

Key Policies:

  • Spoils System: Rewarding political supporters with government jobs
  • Indian Removal Act (1830): Forced relocation of Native American tribes (Cherokee, Creek, etc.) west of the Mississippi River. Trail of Tears (1838) was the deadly consequence
  • Nullification Crisis (1832-1833): South Carolina declared a federal tariff "null and void." Jackson threatened force, upholding federal power. Crisis highlighted growing sectional tensions over states' rights
  • Bank War: Jackson vetoed the recharter of the Second Bank of the United States, believing it was a corrupt monopoly that favored the elite. He "killed" the bank by moving federal funds to state "pet banks," contributing to the Panic of 1837
Key Concept 4.3: The U.S. interest in increasing foreign trade and expanding its national borders shaped the nation's foreign policy and spurred government and private initiatives.

I. Territorial Expansion & Manifest Destiny

Manifest Destiny: The belief that God intended the U.S. to expand its dominion and spread democracy across the entire North American continent

  • Louisiana Purchase (1803): Jefferson bought from France, doubled the size of the U.S.
  • Florida (1819): Acquired through the Adams-Onís Treaty with Spain
  • Texas (1845): Annexed after American settlers revolted against Mexico
  • Oregon Country (1846): "Fifty-Four Forty or Fight!" – treaty with Britain established current northern border
  • Mexican-American War (1846-1848):
    • Cause: Border dispute (Rio Grande vs. Nueces River) and U.S. desire for Mexican land
    • Opposition: Whigs (like Abraham Lincoln) and abolitionists saw it as a pro-slavery land grab
    • Result: U.S. victory. Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo gave U.S. the Mexican Cession (CA, NV, UT, AZ, NM, CO)
    • Impact: Reopened fierce debate over the expansion of slavery into the new territories
