Key Concept 8.1: The United States responded to an uncertain and unstable postwar world by asserting and working to maintain a position of global leadership, with far-reaching domestic and international consequences.

I. The Cold War (1945-1991)

Origins:

  • Ideological Conflict: Democracy/Capitalism (US) vs. Communism (USSR)
  • Post-WWII Tensions: Soviet control of Eastern Europe, Iron Curtain (Churchill)
  • Containment Policy: George F. Kennan's Long Telegram → Truman Doctrine (1947) – support nations resisting communism

Early Cold War Conflicts:

  • Marshall Plan (1948): Economic aid to rebuild Western Europe (counter communism)
  • Berlin Airlift (1948-49): Response to Soviet blockade of West Berlin
  • NATO (1949) vs. Warsaw Pact (1955): Military alliances
  • Korean War (1950-1953): First "hot war" of Cold War; ended in stalemate at 38th parallel

The Arms Race & Cold War at Home:

  • Nuclear Weapons: HUAC (House Un-American Activities Committee) & McCarthyism – anti-communist witch hunts
  • Red Scare: Alger Hiss & Rosenberg cases; blacklists in Hollywood
  • Duck and Cover Drills: Civil defense against nuclear attack

II. Eisenhower & Kennedy Years

Eisenhower (1953-1961):

  • Massive Retaliation: "More bang for the buck" – reliance on nuclear weapons
  • Domino Theory: If one nation falls to communism, others will follow
  • Military-Industrial Complex: Eisenhower's warning about influence of arms industry

Kennedy (1961-1963):

  • Flexible Response: Conventional forces + nuclear options
  • Bay of Pigs (1961): Failed invasion of Cuba
  • Cuban Missile Crisis (1962): 13-day standoff with USSR; closest to nuclear war
  • Peace Corps & Space Race: "New Frontier" – response to Sputnik

III. Vietnam War (1954-1975)

Escalation:

  • Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (1964): Gave LBJ broad war powers
  • Tet Offensive (1968): Turning point – turned U.S. public against war

Anti-War Movement:

  • Students for a Democratic Society (SDS)
  • Draft protests, teach-ins, moratoriums
  • Kent State (1970): National Guard killed 4 student protesters

Nixon & Vietnamization:

  • Gradual withdrawal of U.S. troops
  • Paris Peace Accords (1973): U.S. withdrawal
  • War Powers Act (1973): Limited president's war-making powers
Key Concept 8.2: New movements for civil rights and liberal efforts to expand the role of government generated a range of political and cultural responses.

I. The Civil Rights Movement

Early Victories:

  • Brown v. Board of Education (1954): Overturned "separate but equal"
  • Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955-56): Rosa Parks, MLK emergence
  • Little Rock Nine (1957): Federal enforcement of desegregation

Nonviolent Protest:

  • Sit-ins (1960): Greensboro, NC
  • Freedom Rides (1961): Challenged segregated interstate travel
  • Birmingham Campaign (1963): "Bull" Connor's violent response televised
  • March on Washington (1963): MLK's "I Have a Dream" speech

Legislative Achievements:

  • Civil Rights Act of 1964: Banned discrimination in public accommodations, employment
  • Voting Rights Act of 1965: Ended literacy tests, federal oversight of voting
  • 24th Amendment (1964): Eliminated poll taxes

Expanding Movements:

  • Black Power: Malcolm X, Black Panthers – more militant approach
  • Cesar Chavez & United Farm Workers: Latino civil rights
  • American Indian Movement (AIM): Native American rights
  • Women's Movement: Betty Friedan's The Feminine Mystique, NOW (1966)
  • Stonewall Riots (1969): Birth of modern LGBTQ+ rights movement

II. Liberalism & Conservative Response

Great Society (LBJ):

  • War on Poverty: Medicare, Medicaid, Head Start, Food Stamps
  • Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD)
  • Immigration Act of 1965: Ended national origins quotas

Conservative Backlash:

  • Barry Goldwater (1964): "A choice, not an echo" – limited government
  • Rise of the New Right: Reaction to civil rights, feminism, counterculture
  • Silent Majority: Nixon's appeal to "forgotten Americans"
Key Concept 8.3: Postwar economic and demographic changes had far-reaching consequences for American society, politics, and culture.

I. Economic & Demographic Changes

Postwar Boom:

  • Baby Boom (1946-1964): 76 million babies
  • Suburbanization: Levittowns, Interstate Highway System (1956)
  • Sun Belt Migration: Shift to South and West

Culture of the 1950s:

  • Consumerism: TV, automobiles, rock 'n' roll
  • Conformity: "Organization Man," suburban domesticity
  • Beat Generation: Counter-culture precursors (Jack Kerouac, Allen Ginsberg)

II. The 1960s Counterculture

  • Hippies, "Sex, Drugs, Rock 'n' Roll"
  • Woodstock (1969): Peak of counterculture
  • Environmental Movement:
    • Rachel Carson's Silent Spring (1962) – sparked modern environmentalism
    • Earth Day (1970), EPA (1970), Clean Air/Water Acts

III. Economic Challenges of the 1970s

  • Stagflation: High inflation + high unemployment
  • Oil Crises:
    • OPEC Embargo (1973) – gas lines, speed limits
    • Energy Crisis (1979) – under Carter
  • Deindustrialization: Decline of manufacturing, "Rust Belt"
