Unit 7: 1890–1945

Key Concept 7.1: Growth expanded opportunity, while economic instability led to new efforts to reform U.S. society and its economic system.

I. The Progressive Era (1890s-1920)

  • Causes: Reaction to the problems of the Gilded Age (political corruption, inequality, poor working conditions, social injustice).
  • Key Beliefs: Government should be more active in solving social problems. Use of experts and science ("Scientific Management") to improve society.
  • Muckrakers: Journalists who exposed corruption and problems.
    • Upton Sinclair's The Jungle (1906) – exposed meatpacking industry → Pure Food and Drug Act and Meat Inspection Act.
    • Ida Tarbell – exposed Standard Oil's corrupt business practices.
    • Jacob Riis's How the Other Half Lives – exposed urban poverty and tenements.
  • Progressive Reforms:
    • Political: Initiative, Referendum, Recall. 17th Amendment (1913) – direct election of Senators.
    • Social: Settlement houses (Jane Addams). Temperance Movement leading to 18th Amendment (1919) – Prohibition.
    • Economic: Clayton Antitrust Act (1914) – strengthened antitrust laws. Federal Reserve Act (1913) – created central banking system.
  • Presidents:
    • Theodore Roosevelt (1901-1909): "Trustbuster," "Square Deal," conservationist.
    • Woodrow Wilson (1913-1921): "New Freedom," Federal Reserve, Clayton Antitrust Act.

II. The Great Depression & The New Deal

Causes of the Great Depression:

  • Overproduction & underconsumption.
  • Unequal distribution of wealth.
  • Stock market speculation & crash (1929).
  • Bank failures.
  • Hawley-Smoot Tariff (1930) – worsened international trade.

Herbert Hoover's Response:

  • Believed in "voluntarism" and "rugged individualism."
  • Reconstruction Finance Corporation (RFC) – too little, too late.

Franklin D. Roosevelt's New Deal:

  • Philosophy: Government intervention to provide Relief, Recovery, and Reform.
  • First New Deal (1933-1935): Focused on immediate relief and economic recovery.
    • Bank Holiday & Emergency Banking Relief Act
    • FDIC – insured bank deposits.
    • AAA – paid farmers to reduce production.
    • TVA – built dams for electricity and flood control.
    • NRA – set industry codes for production (declared unconstitutional).
    • CCC – employed young men in conservation projects.
  • Second New Deal (1935-1938): Focused on long-term reform and social welfare.
    • WPA – massive public works employment.
    • Social Security Act (1935) – old-age pensions, unemployment insurance.
    • Wagner Act (NLRA) – protected workers' right to unionize.
  • Critics of the New Deal:
    • Left (Huey Long, Father Coughlin): Said it didn't do enough for the poor.
    • Right (American Liberty League): Said it made government too powerful and interfered with free markets.
  • Impact: Expanded the power and role of the federal government. Created a "safety net." Did not end the Depression (WWII did), but restored hope.
Key Concept 7.2: Innovations in communications and technology contributed to the growth of mass culture, while significant changes occurred in internal and international migration patterns.

I. The 1920s: A Modern Culture

  • Consumer Culture: Rise of advertising, buying on credit, mass production (Henry Ford's assembly line and Model T).
  • Harlem Renaissance: Flowering of African American art, literature, and music (Langston Hughes, Zora Neale Hurston, Louis Armstrong). Celebrated black culture and challenged stereotypes.
  • Lost Generation: Writers disillusioned by WWI (F. Scott Fitzgerald, Ernest Hemingway).
  • Cultural Conflicts:
    • Traditionalism vs. Modernism: Scopes "Monkey" Trial (1925) – evolution vs. creationism in schools.
    • Prohibition (18th Amendment): Led to rise of organized crime.
    • Nativism & Red Scare: Sacco & Vanzetti case, Emergency Quota Acts (1921, 1924) restricted immigration.
    • Ku Klux Klan: Resurgence targeting blacks, immigrants, Catholics, and Jews.

II. The Great Migration

  • Mass movement of African Americans from the rural South to the industrial cities of the North and West (1910-1970).
  • Causes: Escape Jim Crow segregation, violence (lynchings), and seek factory jobs during WWI and WWII.
  • Effects: Transformed northern cities, created new black political and cultural centers.
Key Concept 7.3: Participation in a series of global conflicts propelled the United States into a position of international power while renewing domestic debates over the nation's proper role in the world.

I. American Imperialism (1890s)

  • Causes: Economic (new markets), military (strategic bases), ideological (White Man's Burden / Social Darwinism).
  • Spanish-American War (1898):
    • Causes: Yellow journalism, sinking of the USS Maine, support for Cuban independence.
    • Results: U.S. victory. Gained Philippines, Guam, Puerto Rico. Made Cuba a protectorate (Platt Amendment).
    • Debate: Imperialists vs. Anti-Imperialists (argued it violated American democratic principles).

II. World War I (1914-1918)

U.S. Neutrality to Intervention (1917):

  • Causes: Unrestricted submarine warfare (sinking of Lusitania), Zimmermann Telegram.

The Home Front:

  • Mobilization: War Industries Board, Committee on Public Information (propaganda).
  • Suppression of Dissent: Espionage & Sedition Acts – jailed those who spoke out against the war.

Treaty of Versailles & League of Nations:

  • Wilson's 14 Points: Idealistic plan for peace, including the League of Nations.
  • U.S. Rejection: Senate rejected the Treaty, fearing the League would drag the U.S. into future foreign wars. Return to isolationism in the 1920s-1930s.

III. World War II (1939-1945)

From Isolationism to Intervention:

  • Neutrality Acts (1930s): Designed to keep the U.S. out of future wars.
  • Shift to Aid: Lend-Lease Act (1941) – became the "Arsenal of Democracy" for Allies.
  • Pearl Harbor (Dec 7, 1941): Direct cause of U.S. entry into the war.

The Home Front:

  • Economic Mobilization: War production ended the Great Depression.
  • Women: "Rosie the Riveter" – women took factory jobs.
  • African Americans: Double V Campaign – victory against fascism abroad and discrimination at home.
  • Japanese Internment: Executive Order 9066 – forced relocation and incarceration of Japanese Americans (upheld in Korematsu v. U.S.).

Wartime Conferences & The Atomic Bomb:

  • Yalta Conference (1945): FDR, Churchill, Stalin planned for post-war world.
  • Potsdam Conference (1945): Truman, Attlee, Stalin – issued ultimatum to Japan.
  • Atomic Bombs: Dropped on Hiroshima and Nagasaki, leading to Japanese surrender. Began the nuclear age.

Key Themes for Unit 7:

  • American & National Identity: The U.S. becomes a world power. Debates over assimilation, nativism, and what it means to be American.
  • Politics & Power: The expansion of federal power through Progressivism, the New Deal, and wartime mobilization.
  • Work, Exchange, & Technology: The boom of the 1920s, the bust of the 1930s, and the industrial miracle of WWII.
  • Culture & Society: The clash between modern and traditional values. The transformative impact of the Great Migration and the Harlem Renaissance.
  • Migration & Settlement: The Great Migration and the internment of Japanese Americans.
  • America in the World: The shift from isolationism to interventionism as the U.S. emerges as a superpower.
