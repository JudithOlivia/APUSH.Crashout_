Unit 3: 1754–1800

Key Concept 3.1: British attempts to assert tighter control over its North American colonies and the colonial resolve to pursue self-government led to a colonial independence movement and the Revolutionary War.

I. The Imperial Crisis (1754-1775)

French and Indian War (Seven Years' War) (1754-1763)

  • Cause: Competition over the Ohio River Valley between Britain and France.
  • Albany Plan of Union (1754): Ben Franklin's proposal to create a colonial government for collective security. Rejected but set a precedent for future unity.
  • Outcome: British victory in the Treaty of Paris (1763). France removed from North America.
  • Effects:
    • Colonists: Gained confidence in their military ability. Felt less need for British protection.
    • Britain: Massive war debt → new taxes on colonies. Saw colonists as unruly and ungrateful.
    • Native Americans: Lost a key European ally (France). Pontiac's Rebellion (1763) → Proclamation of 1763, forbidding colonial settlement west of the Appalachians, angering colonists.

British Taxes & Colonial Resistance

  • Sugar Act (1764): Tax on molasses; enforced more strictly.
  • Stamp Act (1765): Direct tax on all paper goods.
  • Colonial Response: "No taxation without representation!" Stamp Act Congress (first intercolonial meeting to protest), Sons & Daughters of Liberty, boycotts, violence.
  • Repealed in 1766, but Declaratory Act passed, asserting Britain's right to tax.
  • Townshend Acts (1767): Taxes on imported goods (lead, glass, paint, tea).
  • Colonial Response: Renewed boycotts, smuggling. John Dickinson's Letters from a Farmer in Pennsylvania argued against "external" taxes.
  • Boston Massacre (1770): Clash between colonists and British soldiers; used as propaganda.
  • Tea Act (1773): Gave British East India Company a monopoly on tea.
  • Colonial Response: Boston Tea Party (Sons of Liberty dumped tea into harbor).
  • Coercive/Intolerable Acts (1774): Punishment for Boston Tea Party.
  • Closed Boston Harbor, suspended self-government in Massachusetts.
  • Colonial Response: First Continental Congress (1774) – called for a continued boycott and preparation of colonial militias.

II. The Movement for Independence

  • Shift in Rationale: Initially fought for "rights as Englishmen," shifted to a movement for independence based on Enlightenment ideals (John Locke's natural rights, social contract).
  • Key Documents:
    • Thomas Paine's Common Sense (1776): Persuaded ordinary colonists to support independence by arguing monarchy was corrupt and it was "common sense" for America to be free.
    • Declaration of Independence (1776): Drafted by Thomas Jefferson. Listed grievances against King George III and justified revolution based on Locke's ideas of "life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness."

III. The Revolutionary War (1775-1783)

Early Phase (1775-1778): Patriots vs. Professional British Army.

  • British Advantages: Strongest navy, professional army, greater resources.
  • Colonial Advantages: Fighting on home turf, motivated cause, George Washington's leadership.
  • Key Battles: Lexington & Concord (first), Saratoga (1777) – TURNING POINT – convinced France to ally with the U.S.

Later Phase (1778-1783): A World War.

  • Franco-American Alliance (1778): Provided crucial military and financial aid.
  • Key Battle: Yorktown (1781) – American/French force trapped British General Cornwallis, leading to surrender.

Treaty of Paris (1783):

  • Granted U.S. independence.
  • Borders: Mississippi River to the west, Canada to the north, Florida to the south.
  • U.S. promises to repay debts to British merchants and not to persecute Loyalists.
Key Concept 3.2: The American Revolution's democratic and republican ideals inspired new experiments with different forms of government.

I. Impact of the Revolution

  • Egalitarianism: Idea of equality spread, challenging old social hierarchies (though incomplete).
  • Republican Motherhood: Idea that women had a vital role in raising virtuous, patriotic citizens. This raised women's status as moral leaders but kept them in the domestic sphere.
  • Slavery: Northern states began gradual emancipation. Southern states strengthened the institution. Contradiction: "All men are created equal" vs. the existence of slavery.
  • Native Americans: Generally lost out. Viewed as enemies who sided with the British. U.S. expansion increased.

II. The First American Governments

  • State Constitutions: Featured weak executives, strong legislatures, bills of rights, and expanded (but not universal) suffrage.
  • Articles of Confederation (1781-1789): The FIRST U.S. constitution.
    • Structure: "Firm league of friendship" – very weak central government. Unicameral Congress with no power to tax or regulate trade.
    • Successes: Won the war, Northwest Ordinance of 1787 (process for admitting new states, banned slavery in NW Territory).
    • Weaknesses & Crises:
      • Could not tax → government was bankrupt.
      • Could not regulate trade → economic chaos between states.
      • No executive to enforce laws.
      • Shays' Rebellion (1786): Farmer uprising in Massachusetts over debt. Proved the national government was too weak to maintain order, scaring elites.
Key Concept 3.3: Migration within North America and competition over resources, boundaries, and trade intensified conflicts among peoples and nations.

I. Western Expansion & Conflict

  • Despite the Proclamation of 1763, settlers moved west, leading to constant conflict with Native American tribes.
  • The Northwest Ordinance set a pattern for orderly westward expansion but also systematically displaced Native peoples.
  • The weak Confederation government struggled to manage these western conflicts or negotiate effectively with Spain & Britain, who held territory on U.S. borders.
Key Concept 3.4: The new republic struggled to define and extend democratic ideals in the face of rapid economic, territorial, and demographic changes.

I. The Constitutional Convention (1787)

  • Goal: Revise the Articles of Confederation.
  • Outcome: Scrapped them and created a new Constitution.
  • Key Debates & Compromises:
    • Virginia Plan (Large State) vs. New Jersey Plan (Small State) → Great (Connecticut) Compromise: Bicameral legislature (House by population, Senate equal).
    • 3/5 Compromise: Slaves counted as 3/5 of a person for representation and taxation.
    • Commerce Compromise: Congress could tax imports but not exports; could regulate interstate and foreign trade.
    • Slave Trade Compromise: Congress could not ban the slave trade until 1808.

II. The Ratification Debate

  • Federalists: Supported the Constitution.
    • Leaders: Alexander Hamilton, James Madison, John Jay.
    • Arguments: Strong central government needed to control anarchy, protect property, and promote commerce. Wrote The Federalist Papers to defend it.
  • Anti-Federalists: Opposed the Constitution.
    • Leaders: Patrick Henry, George Mason.
    • Arguments: Constitution gave too much power to the central government and lacked a Bill of Rights to protect individual liberties.
  • Compromise: The Federalists promised to add a Bill of Rights to get it ratified.

III. Washington & Adams Administrations (The Federalist Era)

Washington's Presidency (1789-1797):

  • Cabinet: Thomas Jefferson (Sec. of State), Alexander Hamilton (Sec. of Treasury).
  • Hamilton's Financial Plan:
    • Assume state debts (to tie wealthy creditors to the gov't).
    • Create a Bank of the United States (BUS) – loose construction vs. strict construction debate.
    • Protective tariff (failed) and excise tax on whiskey.
  • Whiskey Rebellion (1794): Washington put down rebellion against the tax, proving the new government was strong.
  • Foreign Policy: Neutrality Proclamation (1793) in Franco-British war. Jay's Treaty (1794) with Britain (unpopular but kept peace). Pinckney's Treaty (1795) with Spain (access to Mississippi River).
  • Emergence of Political Parties:
    • Federalists (Hamilton): Strong central government, pro-British, industrial economy, loose construction, wealthy/merchants.
    • Democratic-Republicans (Jefferson): Limited central government, pro-French, agrarian economy, strict construction, farmers/common man.

Adams' Presidency (1797-1801):

  • XYZ Affair (1797): French officials demanded a bribe → anti-French sentiment, "Quasi-War."
  • Alien & Sedition Acts (1798): Federalist laws to silence Democratic-Republican opposition.
  • Response: Virginia & Kentucky Resolutions (Jefferson & Madison) argued states could nullify federal laws they deemed unconstitutional (doctrine of states' rights).

Key Themes for Unit 3:

  • American & National Identity: The shift from British subjects to American citizens.
  • Politics & Power: The debate over the proper balance between federal and state power.
  • Work, Exchange, & Technology: The economic causes of the Revolution and Hamilton's vision for the economy.
  • Migration & Settlement: Westward expansion and its consequences.
  • America in the World: The Revolution as an international conflict and the challenge of staying neutral.
